A dead man's switch keeps a machine running only as long as you're holding it. Release it, and everything stops.

This is SPF50 in that packaging.

01 — The Idea

Most SPF products ask you to forgive them for existing. Beige tubes. Clinical language. Packaging that disappears on the shelf and stays forgotten in the bag.

Sun protection is one of the most important things you put on your skin. The category treats it like the least interesting.

The question was: what does SPF look like if it doesn't apologise? What if the packaging made the protection impossible to ignore, the way hazard tape is impossible to ignore? What if the product looked exactly as urgent as the thing it protects against?

02 — The Name

The concept started as KILLSWITCH°. The name was right — urgent, industrial, one word. Then trademark research turned up filed 2021, registered 2022. The US market was blocked.

Renaming a concept at that stage usually means compromise. This time it didn't. A killswitch is something you press to stop a process — active, external, deliberate. A dead man's switch is the inverse: safety is maintained by your presence. The moment you stop, the protection stops.

DEADSWITCH° is the more precise name for a sun protection product. The trademark conflict made the concept better.

03 — The System

The cap carries a prohibition mark in relief, the ° in the wordmark made physical. The body runs hazard stripes: yellow and red, the international language of warning. The label reads UV LOTION | SPF 50, no softening, no category language. The name says what happens if you don't use it.

Red, yellow, black and white. That's the full palette. It doesn't negotiate with the category.

04 — The Detail

Injection-moulded PP. Interrupted thread, radial ribs, 3° draft on all vertical faces.

White-label SPF50. EU Cosmetics Regulation 1223/2009 compliant. EUTM Class 3 — no prior EU registration.

01, Cause of Death

The market was not failing
from a lack of information.

Primary Finding

Men know sunscreen protects them. They don't use it because the product requires them to identify as someone who uses skincare. That is not a knowledge gap. It is an identity barrier, and it is the category's actual cause of death.

Every incumbent has tried to close this gap with dark packaging, sport imagery, and words like "men's expert." None of it worked. The usage gap persists because the underlying product still reads as a cosmetic, it smells like a cosmetic, it feels like a cosmetic, it sits next to cosmetics on the shelf.

The solution is not to argue against that perception. It is to design a product so distinctively non-cosmetic that the category association never forms in the first place.

Contributing Factor 01

Category Language

Words like "lotion", "moisturising", "skin care routine" activate the same cognitive frame incumbents are trying to avoid. The product claims protection but communicates beauty ritual.

Contributing Factor 02

Shelf Positioning

Pharmacy and grocery SPF sits adjacent to women's skincare, face creams, and beauty. Context is brand communication. The shelf kills the positioning before the consumer reads a word.

Contributing Factor 03

Design Language Failure

Dark packaging and sport imagery are a surface-level patch. The format, tube, lotion bottle, squeeze pack, still reads as beauty. Format is the primary identity signal, not colour or typography.

The Structural Opening

Industrial Design Language

No brand in EU men's SPF uses industrial safety aesthetics as its primary design language. That visual vocabulary reads as a tool, not a product. It bypasses the barrier entirely.

STRATEGIC ANALYSIS
05, Strategic Themes

Five forces the brand is built to exploit.

The PESTEL and ecosystem analysis produced five structural themes that define DEADSWITCH°'s strategic position. Each theme identifies a gap, a tension, or a shift in the category that the brand is specifically designed to address.

ST-01

The Masculinity Unlock

The usage gap is not an information deficit — men know SPF protects them. It is an identity barrier. Industrial safety design language (hazard stripe, functional packaging, technical nomenclature) reads as a tool, not a beauty product. DEADSWITCH° bypasses the barrier rather than arguing against it.

ST-02

Regulatory Churn as Competitive Moat

Four UV filters banned or restricted in the EU since 2024. Incumbents face continuous reformulation cost and brand disruption. A new brand built on a compliant-from-launch filter stack turns competitors' compliance burden into a product claim: no compromised ingredients, designed for the regulation others are retrofitting to.

ST-03

Creator as Dermatologist

Social creators with skin-health positioning are replacing pharmacy recommendations as the primary SPF credibility channel. A 60-second TikTok review from a verified creator now achieves what required clinical relationships in 2020. Strategic seeding in outdoor, fitness, and sport verticals reduces CAC while building authentic brand credibility.

ST-04

DTC as Identity Infrastructure

DTC is not just a distribution choice — it is a brand architecture decision. Owning the purchase journey controls the unboxing, the product narrative, and post-purchase communication without shelf adjacency to Nivea and Garnier. Social commerce closes the gap between discovery and purchase in ways retail contracts structurally prohibit.

ST-05

Packaging as the Product Claim

For men who do not read skincare packaging, the container communicates the brand before any copy is read. The industrial format, hazard colourway, and functional material language make the positioning argument without requiring consumer engagement with text. PPWR compliance adds a second claim: designed for the regulation competitors are scrambling to retrofit.

07, Visual Identity

The brand in form.
Every element earns its place.

The visual system is built on the same logic as the positioning: industrial, functional, non-cosmetic. Nothing is decorative. Each element has a specific job, to remove the beauty-category association before the consumer reads a single word.

Brand Identity Elements

Brand Symbol

The prohibition mark, used independently at any scale where the full wordmark cannot fit. Cap hardware, document headers, embossed labels, status indicators. At its most reduced, it is the brand.

Logotype

The full wordmark in a heavily outlined block typeface. Used for primary brand moments: packaging front, OOH, campaign, and any context where the brand needs to be legible at first contact. The prohibition mark is embedded as the degree symbol, mark and wordmark are inseparable.

Colour System
#FFF200
Hazard Yellow
Primary canvas. Industrial warning colour. Occupies the same visual register as site-safety tape, high-visibility workwear, and chemical hazard signage. In a beauty-shelf context, it reads as a tool.
#E8350A
Alert Red
Urgency and consequence. UV damage, sunburn, skin cancer risk. Used for status indicators, action states, and emphasis, never as decoration.
#111111
Technical Black
The colour of industrial documentation, safety manuals, and machinery. Primary text colour. At full weight it reads as authority, not elegance.
#FFFFFF
Document White
Clinical surface. The colour of product data sheets, test results, and compliance documentation. Used as the card and label ground.
Brand Texture, Hazard Pattern

The diagonal hazard stripe is the brand's primary surface texture. It signals construction zones, site safety, and industrial warning in every context it appears, packaging trim, section dividers, campaign backgrounds, label accents. The pattern is always Hazard Yellow (#FFF200) and Alert Red (#E8350A): the brand's two action colours in direct collision. Never used directly behind body text, text always sits on a clean yellow or solid colour panel. The stripe pattern frames and borders those panels as a structural element, marking transitions and boundaries.

The typeface of industrial safety manuals, pharmaceutical labelling, and airport wayfinding. No personality of its own, it gets out of the way of the message. At high weight and large scale it reads as a declaration, not an invitation.

08, Product Line Extension

The tube.
Same brand. Different occasion.

The DEADSWITCH° tube is not a new product, it is the same protection in a different form factor. The jar owns the shelf. The tube owns the moment of use. Both are needed, but in the wrong order they damage each other.

Product, Mark
Format, Tube Cap-Down
Format, Tube Upright
When this launches

Only after the jar has established brand recognition, minimum 18 to 24 months post-launch. The jar is the brand's hero object, it creates the mental model for DEADSWITCH°. Launching the tube too early turns the brand into a format portfolio before it has an identity. The jar must own its space first. When consumers already know what DEADSWITCH° stands for, the tube reads as range depth. Before that, it reads as brand confusion.

Use cases the tube unlocks

The jar is a shelf product, a deliberate purchase with a place in a bathroom cabinet. The tube addresses a different set of occasions: sport and active use where squeeze application beats a jar, carry-on travel at 100ml, gym bag and beach bag portability, and trial-size SKUs as acquisition vehicles for consumers not yet ready to commit to the full format. These are genuinely separate occasions, which means cannibalization risk is low if the positioning is clear.

Pricing implications

The tube must not undercut the jar on a per-milliliter basis. Two viable approaches: parity pricing by volume, same cost per ml at a lower absolute price point because of smaller fill, or a slight premium for an active/sport positioning. Pricing the tube below the jar's per-ml rate devalues the core product and erodes the brand's not-a-basic-sunscreen positioning. The tube should feel like a deliberate format choice, not a cheaper version of the same thing.

Logistics considerations

A tube SKU means a separate fill line, different packaging machinery, different shelf placement and potentially different retail channels. Sports retailers, travel sections and impulse fixtures handle tube format differently to the shelf placement the jar occupies. Two SKUs require separate demand forecasting, separate safety stock, and potentially separate distributor relationships if sport retail is pursued. Supply chain complexity roughly doubles from a single-SKU operation. This is a meaningful operational commitment, not just a design change.

Strategic considerations

The tube is the same brand, same visual identity, different form factor. It should not be treated as a sub-brand or variant line, it is DEADSWITCH° in a tube. SPF variants, SPF 30 or SPF 50+, can follow once the two formats establish category breadth. Jar plus tube plus apparel creates a brand that owns sun protection as a lifestyle category rather than a single product. That is the architecture this brand is building toward, but the jar has to be the undisputed entry point first.
